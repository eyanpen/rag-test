Fiber optic communication is a method of transmitting information from one place to another by sending pulses of light through an optical fiber. The light carries data encoded as modulated signals, enabling high-speed, long-distance communication.

## Core Principles

- **Total Internal Reflection**: Light signals travel through a glass or plastic fiber core by repeatedly bouncing off the inner walls (cladding), keeping the signal contained within the fiber.
- **Modulation**: Data is encoded by modulating the intensity, phase, or wavelength of the light source (typically a laser or LED).

## Key Components

| Component | Role |
|---|---|
| Transmitter | Converts electrical signals into light pulses |
| Optical Fiber | Carries the light signal over distance |
| Amplifier / Repeater | Boosts weakened signals on long routes |
| Receiver | Converts light pulses back into electrical signals |

## Fiber Types

- **Single-mode fiber (SMF)**: Small core (~9 µm), carries one light mode, used for long-distance (tens to hundreds of km).
- **Multi-mode fiber (MMF)**: Larger core (~50–62.5 µm), carries multiple light modes, used for shorter distances (data centers, LANs).

## Advantages

- Extremely high bandwidth (terabits per second)
- Low signal attenuation over long distances
- Immunity to electromagnetic interference (EMI)
- Lightweight and compact compared to copper cables
- Enhanced security — difficult to tap without detection

## Common Applications

- Telecommunications and internet backbone networks
- Submarine cables for intercontinental data links
- Medical imaging (endoscopy)
- Industrial sensing and monitoring
- Cable television distribution
